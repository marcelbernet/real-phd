Slide 48: Rescue Time! What is Dynamical Friction?

If you've made it this far thinking "what on Earth is he talking about?", don't worry. The concept is a bit counter-intuitive, but let's break it down.

The Problem: We observe satellite galaxies that, as they orbit the Milky Way, slow down and spiral towards the center. But in space, there's no air to slow them down! The Earth doesn't brake as it orbits the Sun. So, what is slowing them down? The answer is dynamical friction.

The Explanation (with an analogy):

  1. Imagine a very massive particle (our satellite galaxy) sitting still in the middle of a field of much smaller, uniformly distributed particles (the dark matter halo). The small particles attract it equally from all directions. The net force is zero. It doesn't move.
  2. Now, the massive particle starts moving to the right.
  3. What happens? The particles it leaves behind have spent more time under its gravitational influence. Gravity has had more time to pull on them. As a result, an accumulation of particles, an overdensity, builds up right behind the massive particle. We call this a "gravitational wake."
  4. This "wake" of mass that has formed behind it has its own gravity and... it pulls the massive particle backwards! This pull is what slows it down.

The curious thing is that this force isn't simple. If the satellite moves very slowly, the effect is small. If it moves very fast, it also doesn't work, because there's no time for the wake to form. The braking effect is at its maximum at an intermediate speed, which makes modeling it a real challenge.
